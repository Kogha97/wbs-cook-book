Baklava

History and Origin

Baklava, a sweet pastry consisting of layers of phyllo dough, nuts (typically pistachios or walnuts), and a honey or sugar syrup, has a history that spans centuries and cultures. Its origins are disputed, with some attributing it to the Byzantine Empire and others to the Ottoman Empire. However, it's widely agreed that this dessert became popular in the Middle East and Mediterranean regions. Baklava's intricate layers and syrupy sweetness reflect the culinary exchange along ancient trade routes, making it a symbol of the rich tapestry of flavors found in these regions.

Ingredients

  • 100gr butter at room temperature
  • 1/2 tea cup liquid oil
  • 1/2 tea cup milk
  • 1 piece egg
  • 2 tablespoon vinegar
  • 1/4 tea spoon salt
  • 2 tablespoon yogurt
  • 1 tea cup This
  • 4 water glass Fame
  • 1 water glass starch

Recipe

  1. First, prepare the sherbet for your dessert. Take sugar and water in a suitable pot.
  2. Mix the sugar and water and bring the syrup to a boil.
  3. After boiling, add lemon juice and continue boiling for a few more minutes. Then, remove it from the stove.
  4. For the baklava dough, combine eggs, oil, milk, vinegar, and salt in a mixing bowl and mix well.
  5. Gradually add flour while mixing, and knead the dough until it no longer sticks to your hands.
  6. Stretch the kneaded dough and let it rest for about 30 minutes. This will make it easier to roll out.
  7. After resting, place the dough on the counter and knead it a few more times.
  8. Begin by dividing the dough equally into 3 pieces, then further divide each piece into 9 pieces.
  9. Knead each divided piece from outside to inside, roll them in your hands, and shape them into small meringues. You will have a total of 27 meringues.

Sericaia

History and Origin

Sericaia, also known as Serradura in some regions, is a traditional Portuguese dessert with roots in the southern Alentejo region. Its history is tied to Portugal's colonial past, with the dish evolving from a combination of Portuguese and Macanese culinary influences. Sericaia consists of a velvety, pudding-like custard made from eggs, sugar, and milk, often flavored with a touch of cinnamon or lemon zest. It's typically served with a layer of crushed sweet biscuits on top, creating a delightful textural contrast. This dessert embodies Portugal's history of trade and cultural exchange, resulting in a sweet treat that continues to delight palates both in Portugal and beyond.

Ingredients

  • 1 L Milk
  • 350 gr Sugar
  • 150 gr Flour
  • 540 gr Egg whites
  • 1 Lemon zest
  • Cinnamon powder to taste

Recipe

  1. Start by boiling the milk and the lemon peel.
  2. On the side, mix the sugar and the flour.
  3. Add the egg yolks to the sugar and flour mixture, and mix well.
  4. Add a portion of the boiled milk to the egg yolk mixture, stirring continuously, then gradually add the remaining milk.
  5. Bring the mixture back to a boil to thicken, similar to making custard cream.
  6. Let it partially cool.
  7. Meanwhile, whip the egg whites until they are firm.
  8. In a clay mold greased with non-stick spray, pour the mixture and sprinkle the entire surface with cinnamon.
  9. Cook in the oven at 220°C.
  10. After 10 minutes, use your fingers to make small holes at different points on the surface and continue cooking for approximately 20 more minutes.
  11. Finally, serve the dessert cold.
